Reading Comprehension DELE C2: Strategies

Reading comprehension in the DELE C2 is present in all three tests of the exam. The texts are dense, with specialized vocabulary and complex syntactic structures. It is not enough to understand the words: you must grasp nuances, infer implicit meanings, and relate information from different sources.

Where does reading comprehension appear?

  • Test 1 (105 min): Several of the 6 tasks include reading texts combined with language use exercises and listening comprehension.
  • Test 2 (150 min): Tasks 2 and 3 require reading texts to then produce a written piece based on them.
  • Test 3 (30 min prep + 20 min exam): You read texts during preparation to then speak about them in front of the examiners.

Types of texts in DELE C2

The exam uses authentic texts from various sources:

Academic and scientific texts

Research articles, philosophical essays, advanced scientific dissemination texts. They usually have specialized terminology and complex argumentation with multiple levels of subordination.

Literary texts

Excerpts from novels, short stories, literary essays, literary criticism. They require sensitivity to figurative language, irony, and stylistic resources.

Opinion journalistic texts

Editorials, opinion columns, articles of political or social analysis. They combine factual information with subjective evaluation and require distinguishing between both.

Specialized texts

Legal, economic, technical documents. They have a very specific lexicon and formal structures typical of each discipline.

Reading tasks in Test 1

Text reconstruction task

A text with gaps where you must insert extracted fragments. It assesses your understanding of textual cohesion.

Strategies:

  • Read the entire text first to understand the topic and the general structure.
  • Pay attention to connectors and anaphoric references (pronouns, demonstratives) surrounding each gap.
  • Look for thematic coherence: the fragment must continue the idea of the paragraph.
  • Check the grammatical agreement between the fragment and the surrounding text.

Multiple choice task

You read a long text and answer questions about main ideas, details, inferences, and the author's purpose.

Strategies:

  • Read the questions before the text to know what to look for.
  • Distinguish between what the text says explicitly and what it implies.
  • The correct answers are usually paraphrases, not literal quotes from the text.
  • Eliminate options that contradict the text or go beyond what it says.

Language use task with text

A text with gaps where you must choose the correct lexical or grammatical option. It assesses your command of the linguistic system in context.

Strategies:

  • Read the entire sentence before choosing. The broad context is key.
  • Pay attention to collocations (fixed combinations of words).
  • Consider the register: a word may be grammatically correct but inappropriate for the text's register.
  • Review prepositions governed by verbs and adjectives.

Reading in Test 2

Tasks 2 and 3: Read to write

You read texts (sometimes with graphic data) and must produce a written text that integrates that information. Reading here is instrumental: you need to extract key ideas to use in your writing.

Strategies:

  • Selective reading: You don't need to memorize everything. Identify the thesis of each text, the main arguments, and the most relevant data.
  • Underline and annotate: Mark the ideas you will use in your text. Note in the margin how you will integrate them.
  • Identify the differences: If there are several texts, look for where they agree and where they disagree. That gives you material to contrast in your writing.
  • Do not copy: Always rephrase. Copying phrases from source texts is penalized.

Reading in Test 3 (oral)

During the 30 minutes of preparation, you read texts that will serve as the basis for your oral presentation and the subsequent debate.

Strategies:

  • First quick reading (5 min): Capture the general theme and the stance of each text.
  • Second selective reading (10 min): Extract arguments, data, and examples that you can use in your monologue.
  • Organization (15 min): Structure your presentation with the extracted ideas. Also prepare possible questions from the panel.
  • Do not memorize: Prepare an outline, not a script. You need to sound natural, not recited.

Advanced reading techniques for C2

Multilevel reading

In C2 you must read simultaneously at several levels:

  • Literal level: What does the text say exactly?
  • Inferential level: What does it imply or suggest without stating it explicitly?
  • Critical level: What is the author's intention? What resources does he use to persuade?
  • Intertextual level: How does it relate to other texts or to general knowledge?

Management of unknown vocabulary

  • Do not stop at every unknown word. Try to deduce the meaning from context.
  • Identify the root of the word: prefixes and suffixes give you clues (des-, in-, -ción, -miento).
  • If a word is key to answering a question and you do not understand it, look for synonyms in the answer options.

Speed vs. depth

C2 demands both. Practice:

  • Skimming: Quick reading to grasp the general idea (useful in the first reading).
  • Scanning: Searching for specific information (useful for answering concrete questions).
  • Intensive reading: Detailed analysis of key fragments (useful for inference questions).

Recommended daily preparation

  • Read at least one long article a day from sources like El País, La Vanguardia, Letras Libres, or Revista de Occidente.
  • Practice with texts from various disciplines: do not limit yourself to topics you already know.
  • After reading, summarize the text in 3-4 sentences without looking at it. This trains retention.
  • Keep a record of new vocabulary organized by semantic fields.
